Understanding how exchange rates work is essential for anyone who travels internationally, sends money abroad, or simply wants to make sense of the global economy. Whether you are converting dollars to euros for a vacation or tracking the value of your home currency against the US dollar, exchange rates affect your purchasing power in real and tangible ways.

In this guide, we break down the fundamentals of how exchange rates are determined, the different systems countries use, and what causes these rates to fluctuate on a daily basis.

## What Is an Exchange Rate?

An exchange rate is simply the price of one currency expressed in terms of another. For example, if the USD/EUR exchange rate is 0.92, that means one US dollar can buy 0.92 euros. Exchange rates allow people, businesses, and governments to convert one currency into another, making international trade and travel possible.

Exchange rates are quoted in currency pairs. The first currency in the pair is the base currency, and the second is the quote currency. When you see USD/JPY = 148.50, it means one US dollar equals 148.50 Japanese yen.

## How Are Exchange Rates Set?

There are three main systems that countries use to determine their exchange rates:

### Floating Exchange Rate

Most major economies, including the United States, the European Union, Japan, and the United Kingdom, use a floating exchange rate system. Under this system, the value of a currency is determined entirely by supply and demand in the foreign exchange market, also known as the forex market.

When demand for a currency rises, its value increases. When demand falls, the currency weakens. This happens continuously throughout the trading day, which is why exchange rates fluctuate minute by minute.

### Fixed Exchange Rate

Some countries peg their currency to another currency, usually the US dollar or the euro. Under a fixed exchange rate system, the government or central bank commits to buying and selling its currency at a predetermined rate. For example, Hong Kong has maintained a peg to the US dollar since 1983.

Fixed rates provide stability and predictability, but they require the central bank to hold large reserves of the pegged currency and actively intervene in markets to maintain the rate.

### Managed Float

A managed float, sometimes called a dirty float, sits between the two extremes. The currency generally floats freely, but the central bank occasionally intervenes to stabilize the rate or steer it in a desired direction. China's yuan and India's rupee operate under managed float systems.

## The Foreign Exchange Market

The forex market is where exchange rates come to life. It is the largest financial market in the world, with daily trading volumes exceeding $7.5 trillion. Unlike stock markets, the forex market operates 24 hours a day, five days a week, across major financial centers in London, New York, Tokyo, and Sydney.

### Who Participates in the Forex Market?

The forex market has several key participants:

- **Central banks** manage monetary policy and sometimes intervene directly in markets.
- **Commercial banks** facilitate currency transactions for businesses and individuals.
- **Institutional investors** such as hedge funds and pension funds trade currencies for profit.
- **Multinational corporations** convert currencies to conduct international business.
- **Retail traders** speculate on currency movements through online trading platforms.
- **Tourists and individuals** exchange money for travel or personal transfers.

## What Causes Exchange Rates to Change?

Exchange rates are influenced by a wide range of economic, political, and market factors. Here are some of the most important drivers:

### Interest Rates

When a country raises its interest rates, its currency tends to strengthen. Higher interest rates attract foreign investors seeking better returns on their deposits and bonds, increasing demand for that currency. Conversely, lower interest rates can cause a currency to weaken.

### Inflation

Countries with consistently low inflation tend to have stronger currencies because their purchasing power erodes more slowly. High inflation reduces the value of a currency relative to others, leading to depreciation over time.

### Economic Performance

Strong economic indicators such as GDP growth, low unemployment, and healthy manufacturing output tend to attract foreign investment and boost a currency's value. Weak economic data can have the opposite effect.

### Political Stability

Currencies of politically stable countries tend to be more attractive to investors. Political uncertainty, elections, policy changes, or geopolitical tensions can cause significant currency volatility.

### Trade Balances

A country that exports more than it imports generally sees stronger demand for its currency, since foreign buyers need to purchase the domestic currency to pay for goods. A trade deficit can put downward pressure on a currency.

## Why Exchange Rates Matter to You

Even if you never trade currencies professionally, exchange rates affect your life in several ways:

- **Travel costs**: A stronger home currency means your money goes further abroad.
- **Import prices**: When your currency weakens, imported goods become more expensive.
- **Investment returns**: If you hold foreign investments, exchange rate movements can enhance or diminish your returns.
- **Remittances**: People sending money to family abroad get more or less value depending on the exchange rate.

## The Difference Between Buying and Selling Rates

When you exchange currency, you will notice two rates: the buy rate and the sell rate. The buy rate is what the exchange provider will pay you for your foreign currency. The sell rate is what they charge you to buy foreign currency. The difference between these two rates is called the spread, and it represents the provider's profit.

The mid-market rate, also called the interbank rate, sits between the buy and sell rates. This is the rate you see on financial news sites and currency converters. When comparing exchange services, look for providers whose rates are closest to the mid-market rate.
